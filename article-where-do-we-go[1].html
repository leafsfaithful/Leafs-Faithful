Where Do We Go From Here?

📅 March 10, 2026⏱ 5 min read✍️ Leafs Faithful

The question feels heavier than it ever has.

Through 64 games, the Toronto Maple Leafs sit at 27-26-11, a record that tells the story of a season stuck in neutral. Not good enough to contend, not bad enough for a top draft pick. Yet.

For a fan base that has spent years expecting more, that middle ground can feel like the most frustrating place of all.

The trade deadline didn't exactly clarify things either.

Management moved out several pending UFAs, recouping a second-, third-, and fourth-round pick. Those are sensible moves for a team that needed to recover some assets. But the biggest transaction was sending Nicolas Roy to Colorado in exchange for a 2027 first-round pick.

On paper, acquiring a first-round pick always sounds strong. In reality, context matters. After the Avalanche added Nazem Kadri at the deadline, that pick now projects to fall somewhere between 28th and 32nd overall. That's still a valuable asset, but it's a far cry from the type of premium draft capital that can quickly reshape a franchise.

So the Leafs added picks. They shed some expiring contracts. But they didn't fundamentally answer the question hanging over this organization.

The Matthews Question

Every path forward begins with one player: Auston Matthews.

Matthews is two years away from unrestricted free agency, and this offseason could bring the most important conversation the franchise has had in years. Does he see himself finishing his career in Toronto? Or has the frustration of recent seasons pushed him toward a different future?

The Leafs cannot afford ambiguity here. If Matthews signals that he wants to stay, then management has little choice but to pursue some version of a retool — building around him, trying to reshape the roster while keeping the core piece that makes the team competitive.

But if Matthews suggests that his future lies elsewhere, then the conversation becomes much more dramatic. At that point, the Leafs would have to consider whether to explore a blockbuster trade package. Neither path would be easy. But avoiding the decision would be worse.

The Nylander Situation

The next layer of uncertainty surrounds William Nylander. He remains one of the most dynamic offensive players on the roster. But his place in the long-term vision of this team is far from clear.

If the organization chooses to build around Matthews, Nylander likely remains a central piece. If the team shifts toward a rebuild, however, Nylander could become one of the most valuable trade assets available. Skilled, productive wingers in their prime rarely come cheap, and the return could help accelerate a reset.

Leadership Questions

Beyond the roster, there are also questions about who is steering the ship. With Keith Pelley removing Brendan Shanahan from the picture, the leadership structure suddenly feels less defined than it did before.

Where is the hockey direction coming from now? Is the vision being driven by Brad Treliving? Large portions of the fan base have already voiced their frustration with the current management group, and when an organization lacks a clear and trusted direction, every roster decision feels heavier.

The Knies Rumours

And then there are the rumours. The possibility that Matthew Knies could be moved — especially to a rival like the Montreal Canadiens — has only added to the unease surrounding the team.

Knies represents exactly the type of young player rebuilding teams hope to build around. If his name is truly circulating in trade discussions, it raises serious questions about what timeline the Leafs are actually pursuing.

The Draft Hope

If there is one silver lining to a difficult season, it's the possibility of a high draft pick. A top-five selection could bring in a legitimate cornerstone prospect — players like Gavin McKenna, Jackson Verhoeff, Caleb Malhotra, Alberts Smits, Otto Stenberg, Chase Reid, or Carson Carels could help form the next generation of Leafs talent.

High-end prospects don't solve everything overnight. But they provide something the organization badly needs right now: hope for the future.

The Leafs are entering one of the most important offseasons in franchise history. The direction this team chooses in the months ahead will define the next decade of hockey in Toronto.

Will they retool? Rebuild? Or something in between? At the moment, the answer remains unclear. But the clock is ticking. 🍁
